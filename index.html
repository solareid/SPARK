ESA M-class mission concept

Resolving how magnetised plasmas convert stored energy into accelerated particles and heated plasma.

SPARK uses the Sun as a natural laboratory for one of the central problems in high-energy astrophysics: particle acceleration. By combining γ-ray and X-ray spectroscopy with high-cadence EUV imaging, SPARK will reveal how solar flares accelerate electrons and ions, transport energy, and restructure the solar atmosphere.

The core problem

Solar flares release up to 10³² erg of magnetic energy in seconds, yet the mechanisms that accelerate electrons and ions remain unresolved.

SPARK is designed to close the observational gap that has limited decisive tests of particle acceleration theory: incomplete ion diagnostics, insufficient γ-ray sensitivity, and flare imaging unable to capture the fastest dynamics without saturation.

Science questions

Three questions define the mission

SQ1

How are electrons and ions accelerated during solar flares?

SPARK will compare electron and ion spectra, timing, composition and flare morphology to identify dominant acceleration mechanisms.

SQ2

How is flare energy transported and dissipated?

SPARK will map how energy moves from the corona into the chromosphere and transition region through particles, waves and conduction.

SQ3

How do reconnection, waves and plasma instabilities interact?

SPARK will resolve QPPs, plasmoids, current-sheet fragmentation, supra-arcade dynamics and coronal wave responses.

Transformative capability

A measurement solar physics has never had

For the first time, SPARK will combine time-resolved measurements of the complete non-thermal particle inventory from keV to GeV with multi-thermal plasma imaging at the spatial and temporal scales intrinsic to flare energy release.

This enables a mechanism-level understanding of how magnetic energy becomes accelerated particles, heat, radiation and motion.

Why now?

The science opportunity and technology readiness align.

Large-area γ-ray detector technology has matured for MeV sensitivity, Solar Orbiter/EUI has demonstrated short-exposure high-resolution EUV flare imaging, and Solar Cycle 27 offers the flare statistics needed for population-level tests.

From rare diagnostics to routine physics

SPARK turns γ-ray line spectroscopy and simultaneous flare-core EUV imaging from exceptional observations into a systematic programme for particle acceleration physics.
