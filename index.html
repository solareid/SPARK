ESA M-class mission concept

Resolving how solar flares accelerate particles.

SPARK is designed to answer a fundamental question in astrophysics: how magnetised plasmas explosively convert stored energy into accelerated particles and heated plasma.

By combining γ-ray and X-ray spectroscopy with high-cadence EUV imaging, SPARK will observe electrons, ions and plasma response together at the scales where flare energy release occurs.

The core problem

The Sun is the only high-energy particle accelerator where the accelerator, particles and plasma response can be resolved together.

Solar flares release up to 10³² erg of magnetic energy in seconds, yet the mechanisms that accelerate electrons and ions, partition energy, and drive plasma heating remain unresolved.

What SPARK sees

Particles and plasma in one coherent measurement

LASSAN sees the particles

  • X-rays from accelerated electrons
  • γ-ray lines from accelerated ions
  • Pion-decay emission from relativistic ions
  • Hot flare plasma through thermal X-rays

HiFI sees the plasma

  • Flare ribbons and kernels
  • Hot flare loops and reconnection structures
  • Waves, oscillations and flows
  • Rapid plasma response without flare-core saturation

Together, LASSAN and HiFI reveal the chain from magnetic energy release to particle acceleration, transport and atmospheric response.

Why now?

The science opportunity and technology readiness align.

Large-area γ-ray detector technology has matured, Solar Orbiter/EUI has demonstrated short-exposure flare imaging, and Solar Cycle 27 offers the flare statistics needed for population-level tests.
