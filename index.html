How does the Sun turn magnetic energy into high-energy particles?

Why SPARK?

Solar flares are the nearest high-energy particle accelerators we can resolve in space and time.

From relativistic jets in active galactic nuclei to supernova remnant shocks, non-thermal particle acceleration shapes the high-energy Universe. The Sun offers a uniquely accessible laboratory: a place where magnetic energy release, particle acceleration, and plasma heating can be observed directly.

Science

Three questions at the heart of flare physics

SPARK links high-energy particles to the evolving solar atmosphere, allowing the physical mechanisms of acceleration, transport and dissipation to be tested quantitatively.

01

How are electrons and ions accelerated during solar flares?

SPARK will measure electron and ion spectra, timing and composition to distinguish acceleration by reconnection electric fields, turbulence, shocks and plasmoid-mediated processes.

02

How is flare energy transported and dissipated?

By combining high-energy diagnostics with sub-second EUV imaging, SPARK will map how energy reaches the lower atmosphere through particles, conduction and waves.

03

How do reconnection, waves and instabilities interact?

SPARK will resolve rapid variability, quasi-periodic pulsations, current-sheet fragmentation and coronal wave responses during flare energy release.

Mission concept

A dedicated observatory for the physics of explosive energy release

SPARK is designed to observe solar eruptive events with coordinated EUV, X-ray and γ-ray measurements. From a Sun–Earth L1 orbit, the mission will provide near-continuous monitoring of the Sun and a stable background environment for high-energy spectroscopy.

The observing strategy combines continuous high-energy monitoring with flare-responsive high-cadence EUV imaging. LASSAN provides flare triggers and high-energy diagnostics; HiFI captures the flare core and surrounding corona at the spatial and temporal scales where energy is released.

Payload

Two instruments. One complete view of particles and plasma.

LASSAN and HiFI are designed as a complementary package: high-energy spectroscopy identifies the accelerated particles, while EUV imaging shows where and how the solar atmosphere responds.

γ

LASSAN

Large Area Spectrometer for Solar Accelerated Nuclei

  • γ-ray and X-ray spectroscopy from 4 keV to >100 MeV
  • Electron diagnostics through bremsstrahlung emission
  • Ion diagnostics through nuclear γ-ray lines and pion-decay emission
  • Context X-ray imaging with Co-STIX heritage
EUV

HiFI

High Resolution Flare Imager

  • High-cadence EUV imaging up to 0.5 s cadence
  • 0.28″ resolution for flare kernels, ribbons and loops
  • Four passbands: 304 Å, 171 Å, 94 Å and 132 Å
  • Unsaturated flare-core imaging with multi-temperature coverage
Transformative capability: simultaneous measurement of the non-thermal particle inventory from keV to GeV alongside the multi-thermal plasma response at flare-relevant scales.

Why now?

The science is urgent, and the technology is ready.

MeV detector readiness

Large-area γ-ray detector technologies now offer the sensitivity required to make ion diagnostics routine rather than exceptional.

Solar Orbiter heritage

Solar Orbiter has demonstrated the value of high-resolution EUV and X-ray observations, while also revealing the limitations that SPARK is designed to overcome.

Solar Cycle 27

A 2041 launch positions SPARK to observe a statistically rich sample of flares during the rise and peak of Solar Cycle 27.

Team & collaboration

An international consortium with deep mission heritage

SPARK brings together leading solar physics, heliophysics, instrumentation and data-analysis groups across Europe and international partners. The consortium draws on extensive heritage from Solar Orbiter, RHESSI, SOHO, Hinode, PROBA-2 and other major space missions.

The payload development is organised around two instrument consortia: LASSAN, led by AIP with major European contributions, and HiFI, led by UCL-MSSL with a distributed Co-PI model building on Solar Orbiter/EUI heritage.
